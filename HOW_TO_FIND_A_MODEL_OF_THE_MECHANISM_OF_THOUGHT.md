 HOW TO FIND A MODEL OF THE MECHANISM OF THOUGHT?
 
               BY Maurice DUBOSSON
 
To take up the challenge, one must have a vision in conformity with the reality of the functioning of life, of its birth and of its evolution because thought and intelligence (two extremely linked functions) are always associated with life.
Many authors after Valera and Piaget link cognition and autopoiesis to define the living world. But, their definition of cognition is not clear. Shall we go back to this point later?
First of all, we have to define thought and intelligence in their simplest form because to model such a mysterious and complicated mechanism, we have to get it down to its simplest form.
Let us start with thought. It developed during evolution and to observe its most elementary form, it is logical to search when it appeared in the scale of the living world.
I am one of those who believe that life equals thought and that the first proto-cell has emerged from inert thanks to thought.
Why does thought exist from the birth of life and does not appeared after a long succession of automatic devices? Simply, because we cannot explain rationally evolution in another way. Otherwise, we must admit that RNA existed before the existence of life when it was only created by living organisms, that it would have come from other planets which does not solve anything at all. And the coincidence only cannot explain statistically the complexity and the organization of life. When a beneficial gene emerges an individual, the natural selection of Darwin is without doubt a particularly effective tool for the propagation of this gene in the species. But that does not, nevertheless, explain its emergence in a particular individual! Chance and the external pressures you will tell me! That is a bit too much chance to reach the degree of perfection acquired by our body! What about external pressures then? I cannot see how a phenomenon external to our body can operate on the deepest of our cells to change or to reveal a gene without, an order coming from the interior. But, I shall not extend more these reasons that I have already developed at full length in my short essay “Journal of Thought”.  
Other authors share the same point of view and we can read in:
Life after Kant: Natural purposes and the autopoietic foundations of biological individuality Andreas Weber and Francisco Varela.
 
”Necessary then are the material compounds of an organism, their incessant input and their unhindered supply. But this necessity again is governed by a principle of autonomy – or, as Jonas says, freedom: the fact, that a living system is able to become an ontological center, that it is able to organize itself into a form that is not explainable by the features of the underlying matter (the pure necessity) alone. This autonomy then is nothing other than true teleological behavior.” 
 
Kant that I have just quoted gave us a magnificent definition of thought. According to Kant:              
The mind does not generate things, but structures nature using laws which belong to it, that is to say, using the categories “a priori” of understanding, constituting of the unity of the object. 
With this definition, Kant creates his Copernican revolution. Unlike the Greeks, the world is not harmonious but chaotic and we are the ones who introduce meaning and intelligibility with our mind.
Understanding is the function of the mind which consists of connecting feelings into coherent systems by the means of categories. By categories “a priori”, he meant to indicate pure concepts whose origin was in the mind and not in nature. Kant had even released twelve concepts or categories “a priori” which were articulated with their judgments and in which one can remark among others, the judgments of quantity, quality, relationship, method… and so on.
He continued his reasoning while differentiating:
Knowledge (i.e. the synthesis of what is introduced) taken in its activity which refers to the imagination.
Knowledge taken in its unity which corresponds to understanding.
At last, taking into consideration its totality, knowledge refers to reason.
According to him, our thought is thus equipped automatically with twelve concepts drawn from our understanding which would enable us to select objects from nature.  
 
As Luc Ferry explained so well, the problem of Kant was to get out of the antinomy of Cartesians and the empiricism of Hume. For the Cartesians, they explain the truth (i.e. universality) of scientific laws by the fact that God would have created eternal truths which were already in their head (innate). For the empiricists, there is no idea of the triangle in general, but this idea is particular and temporal for us. Therefore, there are only particular and temporal truths and it is by belief that we make them become universal. For Kant, the concept of the triangle is nothing else but the series of operations which we make over time. All the triangles are particular, but the rule of their construction is universal.
 
Let us move away from this philosophical debate to concentrate on thought which we use to select nature according to our own laws and which come straight from our unity. When an animal selects food and keeps only what is right for it, it makes a choice according to the only criteria it has: “What is good for me?” and “What is bad for me?” In the living world, the choices will be different according to different species. We human beings use words to ask these two questions, but an animal which does not possess language, uses other ways to formulate them. However, the effect is still the same. It makes a choice between what appears good and bad for it. What is good and bad for one are two specifically personal concepts which do not exist anywhere else in nature and, when we affirm “this thing is good for me or this thing is bad for me”, we express a judgment of quality which is one of Kant’s twelve categories. And to reach such a judgment, we must inevitably be conscious of our own unity.
 
How, did the first proto-cell manage to do this without acquiring them from experience?
 
If we refer to Guy Ourisson and collaborators, we realize that life could emerge from inert matter in the form of primitive vesicles which spontaneously developed in water. Let us examine more closely this phenomenon and ask ourselves if thought (reduced to its simplest expression according to Kant’s definition) can exist in such a simple organism. 
At the origin, we have a membrane with dual-layers of polyprenyl   phosphate which delimits an interior environment and an external environment, these two environments being at the beginning identical. So, all the problems are reasonably combined in the membrane. In the absence of the center of coordination, the brain, the membrane must play this role if there is to be a real thought. What are the properties of this membrane? The important thing is the dual-layer. That is to say the hydrophilic heads are in contact with the interior environment and the water whereas the hydrophobic tails are in contact one tail with the other tail. The membrane forms a whole without a gap and can in theory make the difference between an attack which comes from the external environment (its external layer which is disjoined) and an attack which comes from the interior environment (it is then its internal layer which is dislocated). This is very important because this membrane is the only physical element which, by forming a whole, could be fully “aware” of the unity of the system. “I am me” in a way. That thing is good for me, for my unity.
 
Let us keep in mind that memory existed at the molecular level before the existence of thought. The super-molecules, these heaps of molecules made of hollows and bumps are capable of recognizing other molecules. How is it possible? Print of the small molecule on the big molecule? Vibration of molecules with creation of an electromagnetic wave as suggests Pr Montagnier today? 
 
Thought, if it does exist at this stage, would be the result of the movement of molecules through the dual-layer of the primitive vesicles. The membrane of the vesicle is waterproof to water-soluble substances and that is by the way how the constitution of a stable vesicle in an aquatic environment becomes possible. Indeed, the passage of water-soluble substances and water into the interior of the vesicle would involve it bursting. 
However, the system has its other side of the coin. The hydrophobic substances contained in the aqueous environment which surrounds the vesicle dissolve in the membrane and accumulate there. This was proved by certain authors who added some fluorescent organic molecules in the water and who noticed that the membrane quickly became fluorescent. We could have also shown that the spacing of the hydrophilic heads of the dual-layer was different on the external layer and the internal layer, likewise the greatest spacing of the phosphate groups on the external layer makes them more acidic than those which are inside the vesicle. All these distinctive features lead to a preferential orientation of the hydrophobic molecules thus concentrated in the strongly anisotropic environment which constitutes the membrane. For example, a molecule with one of the extremities more basic than the other (a polypeptide) will thus also position itself with its more basic extremity towards the outside. This concentration and selective orientation of the simple molecules, such as derivatives of amino acids, make new reactions possible. Next to one another and suitably positioned, these molecules can indeed react rather quickly, in spite of the absence of a catalyst, and form, for example, peptide chains, first stages on the way leading to the formation of proteins.
This is the other side of the coin. The presence of proteins in the membrane which delimits the vesicle can allow into the interior environment the passage of substances which are not always welcome and which can lead to the disintegration of the system. In other words, the vesicles have succeeded in forming themselves in water and creating an enclosed field thanks to their membrane with a dual-layer made out of hydrophilic heads and hydrophobic tails, but the spontaneous passage into this membrane of hydrophobic molecules can destabilize the system if it is not controlled. It is for this reason that we can suppose the emergence of thought. The opposites and unsteadiness lead to a negative reaction and, consequently, an evolution. If the vesicles had been stable from the beginning, we wouldn’t be here now! 
Before continuing my reasoning further, I would like to specify that these proteins (other than the pores) which in different conditions let pass ions or other small molecules have been clearly identified in the cytoplasmic membranes of our modern cells. They are called ion channels. The only difference with their ancestors is that today, these channels are perfectly controlled and open only in certain circumstances: in the presence of a molecule which comes to stick in a specific way like a key onto one of their receiving sites, or under the traction of the cytoskeleton or opening after a depolarization of the membrane… and so on.
I am about to reach the origin of thought. 
Substance Aa and substance Ba, both from the same biochemical family, naturally cross the membrane of the vesicle via an ion channel made up of a protein perpendicularly positioned to the membrane. Aa helps the interior hydrophilic heads to be stable and thus the coherence of the vesicle. It is good for the system. 
On the contrary, Ba destabilizes the interior hydrophilic heads and can cause the disintegration of the vesicle. It is bad for the system. The disorientation of the hydrophobic tails leads to the deformation of the protein forming the channel and thus temporarily blocks all new penetration of Ba inside the vesicle. This one is safe! Thanks to the tireless repetition of the same phenomenon, the protein ends up associating with substance Ba the deformation of its structure and thus the closing of the channel, a bite the way metals with memory of form always regain the same shape at a certain temperature. At the end of a certain time, the protein which forms the channel, and which has identified the substance Ba because the latter left its print, will thus anticipate the suffering of the vesicle and will end up refusing passage to Ba when this one appears at the entrance of the channel since its deformation leading to the closing of the channel has been memorized and associated with Ba. It is in this anticipation that we can see the first spark of intelligence. According to which principle will it anticipate? Perhaps quite simply according to the principle of the least action noted already in 1744 by Pierre Louis Moreau de Maupertius according to which physical phenomena meet with a main fundamental principle in which nature always chooses, among all the feasible possibilities, the most effective and thus least consuming of energy…
In the end, we have two natural substances belonging to the same family which were arbitrarily selected and separated in two distinct groups (which do not exist naturally!) by a vesicle using criteria which belong to it and which are constitutive of their unity. The concept of what is good and bad for the vesicle, the judgment of quality and the choices which result from it coincide exactly with Kant’s definitions. It is how the first cellular thinking could have been born…
The identity (I am me!) is created by the continuity of the dual-layer. It is the relation between the molecules of polyprenyl phosphate which creates a closed circuit which determines consciousness and generates teleology and the finality to exist. This explains why different philosophers called “will, vital impetus or will-to-power”. It is the motor of life!
 
There was then the crucial passage from a unicellular organism into a multicellular organism. 
One day, under the pressure of environmental factors, the unicellular organism with one cell, by always asking the same double fundamental question “What is good for me” and “What is bad for me ?” ended up considering association with another unicellular organism to form a new multicellular organism. Therefore, the unicellular thought loses its autonomy to become a supracellular thought.
It is at this very moment that the cytoplasmic membrane must have necessarily lost a part of its functions of command in order to transfer them to a supra cellular centre which allowed the unity and the good functioning of the newly created organism. An intense intercellular communication undoubtedly prepared this mutation. This transfer was however not integral since in this multicellular organism, the cell kept certain prerogatives which belong to itself. Throughout the increasing complexity of the system (almost like the Russian dolls which fit together), a transfer of competences must have been happened towards a higher centre, but each time the giver, which was losing a part of its freedom, preserved in guarantee certain fundamental prerogatives to which the new higher centre of decision did not have access.
 
So, we join the authors who spoke about cognition and autopoiesis to define the living world, but we go farther by expressing that it is not the organization of an autopoietic system which is invariant but the organization of its organization. What is invariant is the double question “what is good for me, what is bad for me?” that it asks itself endlessly and that allows it to modify up to its organization.
 
The plan since the emergence of the living world over the inert is always the same:  

Selecting from nature according to one’s own criteria, this is thought.

Anticipation, this is intelligence.

Creation of automatic short cuts, these are reflexes, instinct… and so on.
 
You will notice in this reasoning that everything is only a continuation of logical and purely mechanistic causalities until it is vital to anticipate. The heart of this mystery is thus the principle of least action that numerous physicists use to explain physics including Richard Feynman. Why does it exist while we witness a gigantic debauchery of energy at the beginning of the universe? This is a paradox that I am incapable of explaining and this is why the conclusion of my previous essay «Journal of Thought» is: 
The idea that some have of God, Creator of life, is to be re-examined because his action, if it exists, is pushed back into the logical flow of causalities well beyond the outline imagined by those who believe in it….. If we define God by everything that we cannot explain, it is undeniable that the more we acquire knowledge about the world which surrounds us the more we come closer to Him. This need for spirituality possesses a great number of us, I think, and it is by thought and by knowledge that we shall best satisfy this need.
 
To summarize:
 
For me, first, the fundamental question is: what is good for me? What is bad for me? This question influences the philosophy and the character of every living being. 
 
Second: with a very simple structure like a proto-cell we can imagine how it works. To emerge from inert, the mechanism of life and thought can only be very simple and reproducible (if the circumstances allow it). It's logical to think we can recreate the very primitive mechanism of life in a computer because life and thought are certainly not exclusively the property of organic chemistry. 
 
Conclusion:
 
The interest is to isolate the mechanism of thought and intelligence as simple as possible, mechanism which we can amplify, accelerate and control in order to integrate it then into a system so that it can improve itself the system without any outside intervention. 
This research should be, normally, simple, cheap and suitable to our computer program but we all know that nothing is so simple... Anyway, I do believe it's worth trying.
If we succeed, we solve the problem of the origin of life, we solve the problem of thought, we solve the problem of genetics and we own a model of mechanism we can use to develop science and even artificial life with all the consequences (but that is another problem). And last but not least, we turn upside down philosophy.”
